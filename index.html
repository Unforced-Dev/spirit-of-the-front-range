What is a Bioregion?

(noun): A geographically defined area characterized by distinct ecological features that together form a coherent biocultural home.

Remembering our embedded belonging to the living world invites us to reimagine our social, cultural, and economic systems according to ecological webs of relationships.

Our mutual interdependence is the basis of our mutual responsibility for care and reciprocity.

A Bioregional Commons recognizes the wellbeing of human and natural systems - neighborhoods, watersheds, ecosystems, soil - as shared resources that the people who live in and depend on that land collectively steward.

The 'Extitutional' Hybrid Model

Our bioregional work is held by two complementary structures: a nonprofit membrane that interfaces with existing systems, and a self-governing network that operates autonomously.

Spirit of the Front Range

A backbone nonprofit that helps organize and scaffold the core functions of the Bioregional Commons.

  • Regulatory navigation
  • Resource translation
  • Institutional self-negation

Bioregional Network / DUNA

A self-governing solidarity network - the living membrane through which people of place coordinate, share resources, and tend to our shared home.

  • Autonomous coordination
  • Credible neutrality
  • Mycelial self-organization
  • Distributed governance

Core Ecological Functions

Like a living ecosystem, our network operates through four interconnected functions.

Resource Flow

Flowing resources to the parts of our bioregion that need it most through project funding and mutual aid.

Coordination Tools

Providing tools for coordination, collaboration, and distributed decision-making across the bioregion.

Shared Identity

Fostering a shared bioregional identity - "matriotism" - and facilitating exchange with other bioregions.

Convergence

Convening gatherings, skill-shares, and trainings that deepen relationship and build capacity.

Our Philosophy

We are grounded in a bioregional worldview that understands our mutual embeddedness in this place as the foundation for shared responsibility.

Belonging as Verb

Belonging is not something you acquire - it's something you practice through caring for the land and each other.

Place-Based Solidarity

A post-partisan political identity rooted in place. We are responsible to each other across difference because we share this land.

Commons Governance

Following Elinor Ostrom's principles: clear membership, peer accountability, graduated consequences, and the ability to shape the rules together.

Mutual Support

Flowing resources to projects that serve the ecosystem's needs. Not charity, but recognition that some have more than they need while others don't have enough.

Pluralistic Spirituality

Non-sectarian but not neutral. We recognize organizing as a ritual act while creating space for diverse cosmologies.

Support, Not Control

The nonprofit membrane supports the network but does not control it. Institutional self-negation in service of autonomous coordination.
